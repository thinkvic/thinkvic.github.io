Patrick Kelly and His Fashion World

Jul 4, 2014

I am probably not the best writer on fashion because I spent minimum amount of time on dress every day. But this time, I can’t help writing something about it.

Philadelphia museum of art had an 8-month long exhibition “runway of love” showcasing Patric Kelly‘s work of fashion design. I actually came across the advertisement in the beginning by accident and thought it might be about airplanes? I turned out to be about fashion and I was quite surprised that the designer is from Mississippi, a state I could not be more familiar with.

Not only his clothes really “made me smile,” but also it kindled my interest on this field. I found later online : Actually, Kelly kept his fashion dreams a secret. He said: “In Mississippi you had to be a boy,” “A boy sewing a dress? Oooo-EEEE!” He did not even get a complete education on that subject and Mississippi is not known as the couture capital. Yet, his design swept New York and Paris. I then revisited the exhibition several times to find the answer : What made the French go mad for him? and After 2 decades, PMA still spends 8 months running an exhibition on his work?

The exhibition include five parts: Mississippi in Paris. Two Loves. Lisa Loves the Louvre. Hot Couture. Fast Fashion.

Here are some of his works!

Mississippi has a lot of alligators, which inspired his foax-alligator dress.

About the dress with real nails. He said: “If they don’t want to be in love with you, nail them to the wall until they are ready, then release them.”

Colorful, over-sized buttons are his signature. It’s inspired by the mismatched buttons his grandmother had used to mend his shirts as a young boy.

He never had a commencement ceremony so he decided to hold one for himself at the fashion show.

1989 ready to wear collections. The centennial of the Eiffel Tower and the bicentennial of the French Revolution.

PS.
When a person goes deeply into the soils where he grows on, he could also produce great art. The regional creativity could be a fundamental creative resource and could have universal potential too.

I still remember one friend who worked in the fashion industry said: “oh, it’s a very shallow industry. people are mean.” At least, I now think it’s not that shallow.
