From Phonograph to CD

2015-10-27 archi-neering

When I inserted a dusty old CD into my computer today (my CD drive in my laptop has not been used for over 2 years, gladly it still run), magically some beautiful rhythm from Celine Dion just refreshed the air in my room. Oh, that’s Titanic’s theme song, “My heart will go on.” The music dragged me back to my middle school. That’s the time when Titanic got really popular and I just started learning ABC in English. I remember one of my classmates and I shouting the song in the classroom after school. OK, it also made me wonder how does this magic happen, really?

“my heart will go on and on. Love can touch us one time and last for a life time. And never let go till we are gone” Wait, wait. But how about technology. CD touched us one time and last just a few decades. And we let it go long before we are gone. Haha, this is not a joke but a fact. Music player technology advances too fast before we even have time to review its progress.

Let’s trace it back 200 years to two Thomas.

Firstly 1807, Thomas Young invented a revolving drum chronograph. He described in his Lectures on Natural Philosophy (1807). “His apparatus was a metal cylinder round which was wrapped a sheet of paper coated with lamp-black. The cylinder was turned on its axis, and a bristle attached to a tuning fork placed to trace on the paper the movements of the vibrating prong.”

Then, 1870s, Thomas Edison invented phonograph, which recorded sound onto tinfoil sheet phonograph cylinder using an exposed horn.

For about half of a century, recording was done by this kind of mechanical method, although the storage medium changed to 2D vinyl record.

Finally, electrical domain transformed the music recording and playing devices. Microphone was added and amplification could be realized. The following YouTube videos give you some intuitive feelings.

Oh, don’t forget the jukebox, it’s another milestone! The photo shows the jukebox I encountered a few weeks ago. Bob maintained it very well. It attracted a large crowd, young and old. It also added dynamics to the community. This is not a modern computer or iphone can achieve.

Then we finally have CD, which is based on laser technology.

One thing to note is why it takes so long (half a century) for music recording and playing to adopt electronic tech?

A good argument comes from Brian Arthur’s “coadaptation” and “structural change” theory. The devices cannot just adopt the new tech. Essentially, re-architecting existing structure of economy to make use of the new tech needs a lot of efforts. In this case, it’s accommodating the recording device to the new technology. This is actually the process of new electrical DOMAIN build-out and development. It’s also a process of coadapation with the economy, leading to its structural change. It takes time, decades. This change also DEFINES our time, though.
